# The ENCODE project assigns biochemical functions to 80% of the human genome

## Beyond Genes: Unlocking the Human Genome's Hidden Functions

For decades, the human genome was largely a mystery beyond its protein-coding regions. Scientists previously understood that the vast majority of its nearly three billion bases—the fundamental units of DNA—had unknown functions. While protein-coding genes, which instruct cells to make proteins, were intensively studied, they comprise only about 1.2% of the genome. This narrow focus provided an incomplete view of how the genome truly operates.

The Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE) Project set out to systematically map all functional elements within the human genome. A functional element is defined as a discrete segment of the genome that either produces a defined product, such as RNA or protein, or displays a reproducible biochemical activity, like protein binding or a specific chromatin (DNA-protein complex) structure. Through comprehensive analysis of 1,640 datasets across 147 cell types, ENCODE made a groundbreaking discovery: approximately 80% of the human genome participates in at least one biochemical RNA or chromatin-associated event.

This expanded understanding of functional elements, particularly those outside protein-coding regions, offers crucial new insights into human disease. Genetic variations, also known as sequence variants, linked to human diseases often reside in these non-coding functional elements. For instance, genome-wide association studies (GWAS) typically associate disease with genetic changes across large regions. By comparing GWAS findings with ENCODE's non-coding functional elements, researchers can pinpoint specific putative causal variants more precisely. This information justifies performing whole-genome sequencing, rather than just sequencing protein-coding regions, for rare diseases and for investigating non-coding somatic variants, such as those found in cancer. Understanding these previously hidden functional elements enhances our ability to interpret genetic variation and develop testable hypotheses for disease mechanisms.

## Mapping the Genome: How ENCODE Deciphered DNA's Operating Manual

The Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE) Project aimed to delineate all functional elements within the human genome. For ENCODE, a functional element was operationally defined as a discrete segment of the genome that either encodes a specific product, such as a protein or non-coding RNA, or displays a reproducible biochemical signature, like protein-binding or a distinct chromatin structure.

To identify these elements, ENCODE employed a broad repertoire of functional assays. These included RNA sequencing (RNA-seq), Cap Analysis of Gene Expression (CAGE), and RNA Paired-End Tag sequencing (RNA-PET) to map transcribed RNA regions. Protein-coding regions were identified using mass spectrometry and manual annotation. To locate transcription factor (TF) binding sites, the project utilized Chromatin Immunoprecipitation sequencing (ChIP-seq) and DNase I hypersensitive sites sequencing (DNase-seq). Chromatin structure, which refers to how DNA is packaged, was analyzed using DNase-seq, Formaldehyde-Assisted Isolation of Regulatory Elements sequencing (FAIRE-seq), histone ChIP-seq, and Micrococcal Nuclease sequencing (MNase-seq). DNA methylation sites, which are chemical modifications to DNA, were mapped using Reduced Representation Bisulfite Sequencing (RRBS). Additionally, Chromosome Conformation Capture (3C)-based technologies, specifically 5C and Chromatin Interaction Analysis with Paired-End Tag sequencing (ChIA-PET), were used to probe long-range physical interactions between chromosome regions.

To capture a broad spectrum of biological diversity, ENCODE analyzed a total of 147 different cell types, including cell lines and primary cells. These were organized into tiers: Tier 1 included high-priority, widely-studied cell lines like K562 erythroleukemia cells, GM12878 B-lymphoblastoid cells, and H1 embryonic stem cells. Tier 2 focused on cell types such as HeLa-S3 cervical carcinoma cells, HepG2 hepatoblastoma cells, and primary human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVEC). Additional assays were performed on over 100 other cell types in a third tier.

This ambitious undertaking generated 1,640 datasets designed to annotate functional elements across the entire human genome. Data generation and processing adhered to standardized guidelines, and new quality-control measures were developed to ensure consistency. To integrate these vast datasets, uniform processing methods were applied for each assay, and results were represented both as signal information across the genome and as discrete, computationally identified elements. The project used methods like discriminative training and unbiased approaches to integrate data, establishing a consensus set of seven major classes of genome states, which provided insights into the organization and regulation of genes.

## Beyond 'Junk DNA': A New Map of Genome Activity

The Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE) Project aimed to move beyond the traditional focus on protein-coding genes by systematically mapping all functional elements within the human genome. A functional element is defined as a discrete segment of the genome that either produces a specific product, such as a protein or non-coding RNA, or exhibits a reproducible biochemical signature like protein-binding or a particular chromatin (DNA-protein complex) structure. This extensive mapping revealed that a surprising 80.4% of the human genome participates in at least one biochemical event involving RNA or chromatin in at least one cell type.

ENCODE's analysis showed that a large portion of the genome is actively involved in regulatory processes. Specifically, 95% of the genome lies within 8 kilobases (kb) of a DNA-protein interaction, such as a transcription factor binding site, and 99% is within 1.7 kb of at least one of the diverse biochemical events measured by the project. A key aspect of identifying these functional elements involved mapping chromatin structure and histone modifications. Histones are proteins that DNA wraps around, and their modifications can influence gene activity. By integrating information from up to 12 different histone modifications across various cell types, ENCODE systematically assigned functional attributes to genomic regions.

These analyses allowed ENCODE to classify genomic regions into distinct functional categories based on their unique chromatin states, which are specific patterns of histone modifications and other chromatin features. This classification identified 399,124 regions with enhancer-like features, which are DNA sequences that boost gene transcription, and 70,292 regions with promoter-like features, which are DNA sequences that initiate gene transcription. Beyond protein-coding genes, ENCODE also significantly advanced the understanding of non-coding RNAs and alternative splicing. The project generated a comprehensive catalog of human non-coding RNAs, including small RNAs and long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs), and found that lncRNAs are generated through a pathway similar to protein-coding genes. Furthermore, ENCODE's analysis correlated histone modifications with the inclusion of exons (coding segments of genes) in alternatively spliced transcripts, demonstrating a link between chromatin structure and the process by which a single gene can produce multiple protein variations.

## From DNA to Disease: New Clues in Non-Coding Regions

Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) identify single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), which are variations in a single DNA base, that are associated with human disease risk and other observable traits (phenotypes). While these studies have significantly advanced our understanding, a striking 88% of these disease-associated SNPs are found in non-coding regions of the genome, meaning they do not directly code for proteins. The ENCODE project has provided critical insights into these regions by annotating functional elements across the human genome.

ENCODE's findings reveal that GWAS-associated SNPs are significantly enriched within these newly annotated non-coding functional elements. Specifically, these SNPs show higher overlap with ENCODE annotations, particularly in regions identified as enhancers (DNA sequences that boost gene expression) and transcription start sites (TSSs), where gene transcription begins. For instance, 12% of GWAS SNPs overlap regions bound by transcription factors (TFs), proteins that regulate gene activity, and 34% overlap DNase I hypersensitive sites (DHSs), which are open chromatin regions often indicative of regulatory activity. This enrichment suggests that a substantial proportion of these SNPs are either functional themselves or are located very close to the actual functional variants. Up to 71% of GWAS SNPs have a potential causative SNP overlapping a DNaseI site, and 31% overlap a TF binding site.

Beyond population-level associations, individual genome sequences contain many non-coding variants that lie within ENCODE-annotated functional regions. The number of these potentially functional variants located outside of protein-coding genes is at least as large as those found within protein-coding genes. This highlights that a significant portion of functional genetic variation affecting individuals resides in the non-coding genome.

ENCODE data also enables the association of disease phenotypes with specific cell types or transcription factors. There is a clear correspondence between the type of disease phenotype and the specific cell type or TF involved in the ENCODE assay. For example, several SNPs associated with Crohn's disease overlap GATA2-binding sites and are found within DHSs in immunologically relevant cell types. Another instance involves a "gene desert" on chromosome 5p13.1, where SNPs linked to inflammatory diseases are located near DHSs in immune cells (Th1 and Th2 cells) and TF binding peaks. These variants affect the expression levels of the PTGER4 gene, which encodes a prostaglandin receptor, suggesting a mechanism by which these non-coding variants modulate gene expression.

These findings fundamentally guide the interpretation of sequence variation linked to human disease. By mapping functional elements, ENCODE helps to pinpoint putative causal variants within the large genomic regions typically associated with disease by GWAS. This information, combined with allele-specific data from individual genomes, offers specific insights into the impact of genetic variants. Consequently, ENCODE data justifies performing whole-genome sequencing, rather than just sequencing protein-coding regions, for studying rare diseases and investigating non-coding somatic variants in conditions like cancer. The ultimate goal is to use this functional data to assign every genomic variant to its possible impact on human phenotypes.

## Your Genome: More Active, More Complex, More Mysterious

The Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE) project has fundamentally reshaped our understanding of the human genome, revealing that a vast majority of its sequence is biochemically active. Through systematic mapping of transcription, transcription factor association, chromatin structure, and histone modification, ENCODE assigned biochemical functions to approximately 80% of the human genome. This finding is significant because protein-coding genes, which direct the production of proteins, account for only about 1.2% to 2.9% of the genome, indicating that much more of the genome is involved in regulation and other functions than previously thought.

This widespread activity challenges the long-held concept of "junk DNA," which suggested that large portions of the genome were non-functional remnants of evolution. Instead, ENCODE's work demonstrates that these non-coding regions play diverse and critical roles, particularly in gene regulation. This paradigm shift implies that nearly the entire human genome is a dynamic and complex system, with elements displaying a spectrum of functional properties.

The discovery of widespread non-coding functional elements fundamentally changes our approach to understanding and treating genetic diseases. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS), which link genetic variations to diseases, often identify single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in large, non-coding regions. ENCODE data can now help pinpoint putative causal variants within these regions by identifying their overlap with specific functional elements. This enables researchers to generate testable hypotheses about how genetic variants in non-coding DNA impact gene expression and disease susceptibility, making whole-genome sequencing more justifiable for rare diseases and for investigating non-coding somatic variants in cancers.

Overall, the ENCODE project provides an expansive and invaluable resource for future biomedical research. By cataloging functional annotations and insights into gene organization and regulation, it serves as a foundational dataset. This resource allows for a deeper interpretation of the human genome, guiding studies on human health and disease by providing crucial context for genetic variations beyond protein-coding sequences.
